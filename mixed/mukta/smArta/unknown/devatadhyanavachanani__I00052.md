Description = "RE32402 Devatādhyānavacanāni. The bundle RE32402, which is titled Devatādhyānavacanāni, is a text that focuses on the art of Dhyana (meditation), which in the Vedic and Agamic traditions involves the precise mental reconstruction of a deity’s form to invite their presence into a ritual space.\n\nThe text begins with the fundamental elements of ritual preparation. One of its more practical and unique sections concerns the Nālikera (coconut). The coconut is treated not merely as an offering but as a ritual instrument whose physical characteristics—shape, water content, and the pattern of lines on its shell—are analysed to determine the success or failure of a ceremony.\n\nThis is followed by a profound visualisation of the Yāgaśālā (sacrificial hall). The manual instructs the practitioner to view the physical architecture of the hall as a macrocosmic body. Every pillar, gate, and altar corresponds to a specific anatomical or spiritual limb of a divine entity, transforming the physical structure into a living, breathing sacred space. It is a core point that is a vast catalogue of Dhyāna Ślokas. These verses provide the \"blueprint\" for a deity’s appearance. For a ritual to be effective, the practitioner must visualise the deity with exacting detail, including: (1) Attributes and Weapons - The text lists specific items held in various hands, such as the Pāśa (noose), Aṅkuśa (goad), Triśūla (trident), and Padma (lotus). (2) Vāhanas (Mounts) - It describes the vehicles of the gods, such as the seven horses of Sūrya (the Sun) or the vulture associated with Śani (Saturn). (3) The Navagrahas - A significant portion of the text is dedicated to the Nine Planets. It details not just their appearance but also their astrological \"ID cards\"—their Gotra (lineage), Nakshatra (birth star), and the specific direction they must face during worship to balance cosmic energies.\n\nThe text is inclusive of multiple sectarian traditions, featuring deities from the Shaiva, Vaishnava, and Shakta lineages. (1) Shaiva: Includes descriptions of Sadāśiva, the five-faced form of Shiva, and Dakṣiṇāmūrti, the silent teacher. (2) Shakta: Provides vivid imagery for forms of the Goddess, such as Durgā (described in relation to the buffalo demon Mahishasura) and Bhāgyalakṣmī, who represents various forms of prosperity like Vīralakṣmī (valour) and Śauryalakṣmī (bravery). (3) Ancestral Rites: Toward the end, the text shifts to Pitṛ-Karma (ancestral rites). It contains moving passages regarding the offering of Piṇḍa (food oblations) to ancestors from both the maternal and paternal lines, as well as to \"forgotten\" ancestors or those who died without children to perform their rites.\n\nThe document is a transcription of a physical manuscript, likely palm-leaf (Grantha or Devanagari script). The transcriber has meticulously noted the original folio numbers (e.g., P. 4b, P. 57a) and used a system of brackets and dots to indicate areas where the original material was damaged or illegible. This suggests the text is part of a preservation effort to digitise or record fragile traditional knowledge. The text is incomplete, and the size is 3.4x26.5 cm. There are 59 folios and 5 to 7 lines per folio:"
