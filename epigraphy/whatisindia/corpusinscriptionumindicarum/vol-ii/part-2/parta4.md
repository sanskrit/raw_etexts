Often the person is called ‘protected’ *(guta = gupta, rakhita = rakshita, pālita)* or ‘ given’ *(data = datta)*,[^(\[1\])](parta4.html#_f%0Atn1) by some deity or star ; or the person is said to have some deity as his ‘friend’ *(mita=mitra)* or ‘god’ *(deva)*, or is said to be the deity’s servant *(dāsa)*.[^(\[2\])](parta4.html#_f%0Atn2) In the case of such names as may be called Buddhist, however, words as *saṁgha, dhama-dharma, budha = buddha, bodhi, and thupa= stūpa* appear in place of the deity’s name.[^(\[3\])](parta4.html#_f%0Atn3) It is surprising that such Buddhist names are relatively few, and that there is no marked difference in naming laymen and clergymen.[^(\[4\])](parta4.html#_f%0Atn4) The non-religious names referring to the appearance of the body, mental dispositions, plants or animals are comparatively seldom met with.[^(\[5\])](parta4.html#_f%0Atn5)
